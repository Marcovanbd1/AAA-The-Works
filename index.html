Thinking about neighbourhood

When people say they’re “thinking about neighbourhood”, they’re usually sensing the same thing. Places are being asked to notice pressure earlier, respond more humanly, and hold things together when demand or disruption hits.

This page sets out a simple way to think about what a neighbourhood actually needs in order to do that well. Not a product. Not a platform. A small set of civic capabilities that build on each other.

The anatomy

Our Digital Offer

A neighbourhood needs more than a single website or front door. It needs multiple, everyday digital surfaces that meet people where they already are and lower the effort needed to find support.

This is about inclusion and reach, not optimisation. Different moments. Different levels of confidence. Different ways of arriving.

This is not a new system, not a replacement for clinical tools, and not a communications exercise.

What emerges

Neighbourhood Signals

Once the anatomy exists, something new becomes possible. A neighbourhood can begin to notice patterns of pressure building early, across many small interactions.

This is collective awareness, not individual monitoring. Patterns of need, not people.

Neighbourhood Signals can’t be installed or owned. They only emerge when a place chooses to listen in the right way, with clear governance and restraint.

This is not a dashboard of individuals, not predictive risk scoring, and not something any one organisation controls.

For places that want to go deeper, there’s a longer explanation of Neighbourhood Signals here.

The voice

Support Playlists

This is what people experience directly. When someone says what’s going on, they receive a short, safe, non-clinical set of approved support options.

No diagnosis. No advice. No automation of judgement.

Support Playlists can live inside a full neighbourhood anatomy, or within a credible INT-style website, as long as clear red lines are respected.

On their own, Support Playlists are a voice, not neighbourhood intelligence.

A further civic capability

Neighbourhood Resilience

Neighbourhood resilience is about preparing a place for prolonged disruption, not just short incidents. Power outages, loss of connectivity, system failures, or sustained pressure that lasts days rather than hours.

It asks a simple but serious question: what happens to this neighbourhood if normal routes stop working tomorrow?

When the anatomy is in place, Signals have built awareness, and Support Playlists provide trusted guidance, a neighbourhood can maintain continuity even when digital or institutional systems degrade.

This is not an emergency response or a major incident plan. It’s civic continuity under strain.

How places get there

There’s no rollout sequence or checklist. Places start by building the anatomy, agreeing the red lines, and growing capability in the open.

Intelligence is earned over time, not switched on.

If this framing is useful, that’s the point of sharing it. It’s designed to help places orient themselves before jumping to solutions.
